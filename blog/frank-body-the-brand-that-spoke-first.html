Frank Body: The Brand That Spoke First
- And Won Because Of It

Katherine Chalhoub · 2 April 2026 · 7 min read

Most beauty brands start from the same place

They lead with ingredients, claims, and outcomes. They sound polished. They sound credible. And they sound exactly the same.

Frank Body didn't try to outplay that system. They ignored it.

The real strategy wasn't the product

On the surface, Frank Body is simple. A coffee scrub. Natural ingredients. Direct-to-consumer. There was nothing particularly complex about the product itself.

What made it different was how it was framed. Because while competitors were talking about "rejuvenation," "radiance," and "clinical results," Frank Body was saying: "Hey babe."

That shift matters more than it seems.

The power of first-person brand voice

Frank Body didn't just create a tone of voice. They created a character. "Frank" wasn't a brand talking to you. Frank was talking as himself. Flirty. Cheeky. A little inappropriate. And most importantly - human.

This wasn't accidental. The founders were actively rejecting the way traditional beauty brands communicated - overly polished, inflated, and impersonal. So they did the opposite. They made the brand conversational, self-aware, and slightly irreverent. And they committed to it fully.

"Most brands try to sound credible. Frank Body chose to sound relatable. And in doing so, became credible anyway."

Why this worked (from a brand perspective)

First-person voice does something most brands struggle to achieve: it collapses distance. Instead of brand speaking to customer, it becomes person speaking to person. Which changes everything.

It builds familiarity faster, lowers scepticism, and increases memorability. And in a category built on trust, that matters. Most brands try to sound credible. Frank Body chose to sound relatable. And in doing so, became credible anyway.

The risk most brands wouldn't take

Let's be clear - this approach is not safe. A voice like Frank Body's doesn't appeal to everyone, risks being polarising, and can feel too casual for the category. But that's exactly why it worked.

Because strong brands are not built on consensus. They're built on preference. Frank Body understood that if no one dislikes it, no one really cares about it. So they leaned into personality. Hard.

Instagram wasn't a channel - it was the engine

Timing played a role. But it wasn't luck. Frank Body didn't just "use" Instagram. They understood what it was becoming.

In the early 2010s, Instagram wasn't yet saturated with brands. It was visual, community-led, and behaviour-driven. And Frank Body built directly into that. They encouraged customers to share content, tag the brand, and participate in the experience. This wasn't content marketing. It was community creation. And it scaled fast.

The brand effectively built its customer base through Instagram, turning user-generated content into its primary growth driver.

The genius of UGC (before it was overused)

User-generated content is standard now. At the time, it wasn't. Frank Body turned customers into creators, advocates, and distribution channels - by doing one thing well: they made the product shareable. Not just functionally - but culturally.

Putting coffee scrub on your body is messy, visual, and slightly ridiculous. Which made it perfect for social. Then they layered tone on top - hashtags, captions, community language (#thefrankeffect) - and suddenly the brand wasn't creating content. The audience was. At scale.

"Competitors replicated the product. But not the brand. And brand is what people remember."

What most brands misunderstand about this success

They look at Frank Body and think: "We need a fun tone of voice. We need UGC. We need to be on social." That's not the takeaway.

The takeaway is this: everything was aligned. Product - inherently shareable. Voice - designed for social. Platform - chosen early and intentionally. Audience - clearly understood. Nothing was disconnected.

Why the brand held (even as competitors entered)

Frank Body wasn't the only coffee scrub. And it wasn't the last. But it outlasted copycats. Because competitors replicated the product - but not the brand. And brand is what people remember. Even when celebrity-backed competitors entered the market, Frank Body sustained growth because of the strength of its brand - not just the product.

The deeper lesson

Frank Body didn't just build a product. They built a voice, a point of view, and a way of interacting. And they did it early - before the category adapted, before Instagram matured, before "brand personality" became a requirement.

What this means for brands now

You can't replicate Frank Body by being cheeky, using first-person tone, or running UGC campaigns. That moment has passed. But the principle hasn't.

  1. Your brand needs a clear voice - not just a tone. Something distinct enough to recognise instantly.
  2. Platforms should shape your execution - not just distribute it.
  3. Community is built - not captured. And it starts with participation.
  4. Differentiation must be intentional - not aesthetic.

Key takeaways

  • Frank Body's advantage was brand, not product
  • First-person voice reduced distance and built connection
  • Early Instagram adoption created momentum and community
  • UGC worked because the product and brand were designed for it
  • Alignment - not tactics - drove growth

Most brands focus on what they sell. The ones that scale define how they're experienced.
