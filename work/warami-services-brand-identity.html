Warami Services: Building a Brand That Opens Procurement Doors

Katherine Chalhoub · April 2026 · Brand Strategy • Tone of Voice • Identity • Messaging
Case Study
Warami Services
Brand Strategy Brand Identity Marketing Assets

Indigenous-owned. Multi-trade. Procurement-ready from day one. Here’s how a discovery session, competitor research, brand strategy, tone of voice guidelines, and a full messaging framework produced a brand that gave a new trade services company the credibility to compete for government and commercial contracts immediately.

The Brief

Tom Bowler and Guy Carter founded Warami Services with a strong operational foundation — licensed across electrical, plumbing, carpentry, and fire solutions — but no brand to match. In procurement-led markets, where decisions are often made before a single meeting, that gap costs real revenue.

They came to me needing more than a logo. They needed a brand that would signal credibility, authenticity, and capability simultaneously — to government procurement teams, Tier 1 contractors, and commercial builders who receive dozens of submissions from companies that look and sound the same.

The name Warami, understood as “hello” or “welcome” in local Indigenous language, wasn’t just a name. It reflected a philosophy: engage with respect, deliver with certainty. That meaning needed to live in every touchpoint of the brand — not just visually, but in how the company speaks.

Discovery: What the Brand Had to Do

Before any visual or copy work, I ran a recorded discovery session with Tom and Guy to understand what the brand was truly standing for — and what it had to overcome in the market.

Three things became immediately clear:

The professional credibility gap was real. In procurement environments, first impressions happen on paper — through a tender submission, a capability statement, an email signature. A weak brand translates directly to a weak submission. Warami needed to look and sound like they’d been operating at this level for a decade, not a year.

Indigenous ownership was both an advantage and a responsibility. Supply Nation registration and verified Indigenous ownership are genuine procurement advantages in today’s market. But leaning into credentials without the operational credibility to support them undermines trust. The positioning had to be authentic — grounded in the actual values and capability of the business — not performative.

The multi-trade model was undervalued. The ability to coordinate across four licensed disciplines from a single partner is a genuine operational advantage — fewer contractors, cleaner coordination, reduced risk. But only if the brand clearly communicates it as a feature, not an afterthought.

“Most trade brands sound like they’re trying to win the job. Warami needed to sound like they already had it.”

Competitor Research: A Market of Sameness

The trade services sector has a significant branding problem. Most businesses in this space look and sound identical: blue and white palettes, hard hat iconography, generic safety messaging. “Reliable. Trusted. Local.” The language is indistinguishable.

Indigenous trade businesses face a compounding challenge. Many are small, family-run operations that haven’t invested in brand infrastructure — which means they struggle to compete in professional procurement environments even when their on-the-ground capability is strong. The presentation doesn’t match the capability.

On the other side, the large Tier 1 contractors have brand presence but zero Indigenous credentials — and in markets where Indigenous engagement outcomes are increasingly mandated, that’s a meaningful gap.

This created a clear and unoccupied position: there was no Indigenous trade services company presenting at a genuine corporate standard. The space was open.

Positioning Strategy

The strategic positioning that emerged from research and discovery was precise: Warami bridges the gap between operational delivery, professional presentation, and Indigenous ownership at a corporate standard.

Where competitors are either too informal or too cold and corporate, Warami occupies a distinct and defensible middle ground — warm in its values, rigorous in its presentation.

The brand descriptor we landed on — Technical Asset Solutions — is deliberate. It speaks the language of procurement and asset management. It positions Warami as a solutions provider, not a tradesperson. It signals capability before a single slide is read.

Credible Structured, professional, procurement-ready from day one
Capable Multi-trade, licensed, and coordinated across disciplines
Aligned Supply Nation registered, NSW ICC accredited, Indigenous-owned

Brand Voice & Tone of Voice

One of the most overlooked elements in brand work for service businesses is voice. Visuals attract attention; language builds trust. For Warami, getting the voice right was especially important — because the brand needed to communicate with very different audiences without sounding inconsistent.

The voice guidelines we developed are structured around four core attributes, each defined with a clear “is” and “is not” so the language stays precise in practice:

Assured
Is: Confident, direct, grounded in evidence
Not: Boastful, superlative-heavy, or aggressive
Clear
Is: Plain language, specific, easy to act on
Not: Jargon-filled, vague, or over-qualified
Respectful
Is: Warm, considered, culturally grounded
Not: Deferential, apologetic, or performative
Purposeful
Is: Every word earns its place
Not: Filler copy, corporate padding, or buzzwords

The tone shifts depending on context — capability statements and tender documents lean more formal and evidence-led; email communications and LinkedIn content carry more warmth — but the four attributes remain constant throughout.

Messaging Framework

Beyond positioning, Warami needed a clear messaging framework to guide how they communicate in different contexts. A capability statement speaks to procurement officers. A LinkedIn post reaches industry peers. An email to a project manager operates at a different register entirely.

The framework maps three audience contexts to specific message priorities:

Audience What they need to know How Warami says it
Government & procurement teams Compliance credentials, capability range, reliability record Lead with Supply Nation + NSW ICC accreditation, multi-trade licensing, and the single-partner coordination advantage. Formal, structured, evidence-led.
Tier 1 contractors & builders Speed of mobilisation, trade breadth, professional coordination Emphasise operational efficiency: one point of contact, multi-discipline execution, no handover gaps. Direct and practical.
Asset owners & facility managers Long-term reliability, accountability, quality assurance Focus on ongoing asset management capability and the value of a trusted partner across the asset lifecycle. Warmer tone, relationship-led.

The framework also defines what Warami does not say: no generic safety claims without context, no credential-dropping without substance to back it up, and no language that distances the business from its Indigenous identity. Authenticity is a competitive advantage here, not a risk to manage.

Creative Direction

The visual identity needed to do serious heavy lifting. It had to communicate credibility and warmth simultaneously — and look immediately different from the sea of blue-and-white trade businesses.

Palette. Deep navy (#1B2A4A) as the primary — authoritative, serious, and instantly distinctive in a category dominated by safety colours. Gold (#D4A843) as the accent — warmth, quality, and a nod to premium positioning. Sky blue (#6B9BD2) for clarity and trust, used more sparingly as a tertiary.

Typography. DM Serif Display for headings — contemporary and premium without being cold. Inter for body copy — clean, highly legible, at home in both digital and print. The combination reads as established and considered rather than assembled quickly.

Logo mark. The Warami circle mark — twelve outer blue dots surrounding twelve inner gold dots, with a white centre — draws quietly from the circular motifs common in Indigenous art traditions. It works at every scale: large format print, email signature, social profile. It doesn’t announce itself; it rewards attention.

Brand guidelines. A 12-slide brand guidelines deck codified everything — colour values, typography hierarchy, logo usage rules, spacing and layout principles, tone of voice, and photography direction. The goal was a document the founders could hand to any supplier or collaborator and have the brand reproduced consistently from day one.

What This Shows About Brand

Warami’s project illustrates something I see consistently in early-stage businesses: the gap between operational capability and brand presentation is often wider than founders realise. And in procurement-led industries — where you are regularly judged before a conversation happens — that gap costs real, traceable revenue.

The brand work didn’t change what Warami does. It changed how Warami is seen — and how they sound. In the markets they’re targeting, that distinction is the difference between getting invited to tender and being passed over entirely.

There’s also something worth noting about Indigenous business branding specifically. The temptation is to either lead so heavily with credentials that the operational capability gets buried, or to under-reference identity for fear of being perceived as trading on it alone. The answer isn’t a safe middle ground — it’s to make both the identity and the capability undeniable, simultaneously. That’s what good brand strategy does.

“The brand gave Warami a platform to stand on — not just a logo to stand behind.”
